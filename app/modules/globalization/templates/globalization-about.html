About Globalization

In some external resources you might see various terms associated with globalization used interchangeably. But at Pitney Bowes, these terms mean specific things.

“Globalization” is a broad term that refers to the entire set of activities involved in making a product ready for markets outside of the U.S. “Internationalization” refers to a sub-set of activities that focuses on isolating the parts of your product that will need to be adapted for non-U.S. markets. These activities include “Regionalization” and “Localization.”

Here’s a deeper look at what these concepts mean:

Globalization

  • Considers all world markets as potential audiences
  • Helps ensure that your product is both culture- and language-neutral
  • Is critical to a successful multi-cultural product release and should be undertaken early
  • Consists of many activities that touch various aspects of the product development process and experience for end users

Internationalization

  • Is the first step in the process of globalizing your product, helping you to prepare your product for specific regions and countries
  • Is focused on isolating the parts of your product that will vary from the U.S. version in terms of standards, language, conventions, etc.
  • Touches on all aspects of development, from initial design to building to testing
  • Encompasses and informs two sub-activities: regionalization and localization

Regionalization

  • Identifies region- or country-specific requirements
  • Requires help from local subject matter experts
  • Helps ensure that your product meets various regional and country legislative, legal or other requirements, such as privacy or data storage laws

Localization

  • Typically refers to language-related activities
  • Involves translation of UI elements, errors, help content, installers and other documentation from English to languages defined during regionalization planning

Globalization is a one-time investment you make early — before you write your first line of code — to save your team from having to make a much larger investment later on. While you’ll still need to perform regionalization and localization work for each new country release, you’ll find it easier, faster and more economical to do so with a solid globalization strategy in place first.
